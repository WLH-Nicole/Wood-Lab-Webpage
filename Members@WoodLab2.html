Dr. Levon Abrahamyan

My research interests are broad and fall within two main categories: the molecular study of virus-host interaction and the impact of viral and host genetic variation on HIV-1 pathogenesis and molecular epidemiology. HIV-1 has an extremely high genetic variation that contributes to generation of quasi-species. HIV-1 co−receptor switching from CCR5 to CXCR4 accompanies disease progression in 50% of individuals infected with HIV-1 subtype B. However, in patients infected with HIV-1 subtype C, co-receptor switching is delayed or rare. In particular, I am interested in understanding how genetic differences contribute to the vertical transmission of the virus. Therefore, the biological characterization of HIV-1 envelope from mothers and infants is an important task to pursue. In recent years, it has become apparent that HIV-1 accessory proteins (Vif, Vpr and Vpu) modulate host anti-viral response by using similar mechanisms. However, very little is known about genetic diversity and biological properties of clade C HIV-1 accessory proteins. Therefore, I am interested in the genetic polymorphism and functional roles of subtype C HIV-1 accessory proteins in HIV-1 pathogenesis. These biological differences would be critical to clinical management and antiretroviral drug resistance studies. Additionally, I am interested in developing improved diagnostic capabilities and detection techniques for emerging and endemic pathogens.

Dr. Wan-Ling Hsu

I obtained a PhD in Animal Breeding and Quantitative Genetics with genetics, statistics and programming background. I am working at the NCV for gaining more training and experience in bioinformatics and computing biology so that I can be a developer of tools for analysis of genomic and proteomic data. Projects I am involved are:

  1. HIV drug resistance project:  Study and develop a pipeline for analyzing the raw deep sequencing data (454 GS-FLX Titanium) of low-abundance HIV-1 subtype C drug-resistance mutations in Zambian individuals.
  2. IRF evolution project: Study and identify sequence variations and unique molecular patterns of the IRF family and IFNs among species representing different stages in evolution.
  3. HIV drug resistance project: Analyze the direct sequencing data of HIV-1 subtype C drug-resistance mutations in Zambia patients.

     Help (data mining or bioinformatics):

  1. HH8 – ORF project: Search all known ORFs on KSHV – HH8 genome and their annotated information and functional information for studying and identifying unknown ORFs      
  2. RNA and protein interaction project: Identify IRESs and special RNA structures in the viral genome and predict their potential roles of viral genome replications.
  3. Mechanism of human Ubc9 regulate autophagy project: Search sumoylation site predictions on human autophagy protein sequences.

Landon Olp

I am currently working toward my PhD at UNL. My research is focused on gaining a better epidemiological and mechanistic understanding of KSHV transmission to young children in a KSHV endemic region with high HIV-1 prevalence, such as Zambia. To accomplish this, I aim to identify the persons involved as potential KSHV transmission sources to children. Additionally, I seek to assess the impact of anti-retroviral therapy on the risk of KSHV infection, transmission, and progression to Kaposi's sarcoma in HIV-1 positive children.

Ashley Clark

I am from Columbus, Ohio. I have a BS in biology and undergraduate research experience in microbiology, biochemistry, and organic chemistry. I moved to Lincoln, NE to pursue a PhD in virology. With Dr. Charles Wood as my mentor, I will be working on the NeuroAIDS project to help characterize the neuropathogenesis of HIV-1 clade C. My research will focus on three specific aims:

*To determine whether opportunistic infection plays a role in viral infiltration of the brain.

*To determine if the incidence of opportunistic infection in brain is associated with HIV infiltration and disease progression.

*To determine if there are relationships between neuroinflammatory response, meningitis, opportunistic infection, and HIV-1 infection.

I will be looking at a wide range of opportunistic infections: DNA viruses, RNA viruses, bacteria, parasites, and fungi. Techniques used will include nested PCR, qPCR, immunohistochemistry, in situ hybridization, various histological stains, direct sequencing, ultra-deep pyrosequencing, cryo-cracking, western/southern/northern blot, etc…
